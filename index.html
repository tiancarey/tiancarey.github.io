We welcome enquiries from motivated researchers interested in two-dimensional materials, composites, printed and wearable electronics, sensors, scalable nanomaterial processing and are open to other areas.

PhD/Masters Opportunities

Prospective PhD and masters researchers may join the group through funded studentships or by applying for an external postgraduate scholarship. Relevant funding routes may include Research Ireland postgraduate scholarships, university-funded studentships and international scholarship programmes.

If contacting the group, please prepare:

  • Your proposed funding route and expected application deadline
  • A sentence on why you want to join this group specifically
  • A sentence your research interests
  • Details of laboratory, device-fabrication experience or publications are a bonus but not necessary to start a PhD

Please note that I receive several emails a day. I try to reply to every candidate if you have provided the above; however, if I missed your initial enquiry, I apologise. Please email me again, i might have missed it. The shorter the email the better.

Postdoctoral Opportunities

Researchers interested in joining the group as postdoctoral fellows are encouraged to contact me well in advance of the relevant funding deadline. I can support top candidates in developing external fellowship proposals, as I have significant reviewer experience at the EU and national levels.

Potential fellowship routes include:

  • Marie Skłodowska-Curie Postdoctoral Fellowships
  • Research Ireland Postdoctoral Programmes
  • Research Ireland Enterprise Fellowship
  • Research Ireland Pathway Programme
  • European Research Council and Horizon Europe projects
  • Relevant international mobility and national fellowship schemes

Postdoctoral applicants should normally provide the 3-4 sentences above that are requested for PhD students. Including a clear explanation of how the proposed work complements the group's expertise and how the group can also help your research.

Team

The Research Group develops scalable low-dimensional materials, printed electronics, flexible devices and next-generation electronic technologies. Our interdisciplinary research spans physics, materials science, chemistry and engineering, with a focus on translating fundamental materials research into practical electronic systems.

Group Leader/Principal Investigator

Dr Tian Carey

Research Ireland–Royal Society Research Assistant Professor

Dr Tian Carey is a Research Ireland–Royal Society Research Assistant Professor in the School of Physics at Trinity College Dublin, where he leads the NanoSystems Group. He gained his PhD in Engineering from the University of Cambridge in 2018. He also holds an MPhil in Micro and Nanotechnology Enterprise from the University of Cambridge and and a first class honors BSc (Hons) in Physics with Astronomy and Space Science from University College Dublin. Following his doctoral studies, Dr Carey was awarded a Royal Academy of Engineering Enterprise Fellowship and Marie Skłodowska-Curie Individual Fellowship before securing a Research Ireland–Royal Society University Research Fellowship to establish his independent research programme. His work has been published in leading journals including Nature Electronics and Nature Communications, and his research has contributed to some of the first printed circuits with 2D materials along with a deeper understanding of the charge transport and exfoliation mechanisms of 2D materials. Dr Carey has secured over €2 million in competitive research funding as principal investigator national and international funding agencies and is actively hiring to expand the group.

Research

The Carey Research Group develops scalable two-dimensional materials for next-generation printed electronics. Our research spans the entire technology pipeline, from materials synthesis and processing to high-performance electronic devices, sensors and integrated circuits. We combine materials science, physics, chemistry and engineering to enable low-cost, scalable electronic technologies.

Scalable 2D Materials

We develop scalable approaches for producing high-quality two-dimensional materials and investigate how processing influences their structural, optical and electronic properties. Our work focuses on electrochemical exfoliation, liquid-phase processing and large-area assembly of nanosheet networks for printable electronics.

Selected publications

  • Nature Communications (2025) – Electronic properties and circuit applications of networks of electrochemically exfoliated 2D nanosheets
  • npj 2D Materials and Applications (2022) – Cyclic production of biocompatible few-layer graphene ink with in-line shear mixing
  • 2D Materials (2025) – Assessing quality and purity of MoS₂ nanosheets by diffuse reflectance IR spectroscopy

Printed Electronics and Transistors

We design and manufacture printed electronic devices using solution-processable semiconductors. Current research includes high-mobility transistors, complementary logic, flexible electronics, fibre electronics and integrated circuits fabricated using scalable printing technologies.

Selected publications

  • Nature Communications (2017) – Fully inkjet-printed two-dimensional material field-effect heterojunctions for wearable and textile electronics
  • Advanced Electronic Materials (2021) – Inkjet Printed Circuits with 2D Semiconductor Inks for High-Performance Electronics
  • ACS Nano (2023) – High-Mobility Flexible Transistors with Low-Temperature Solution-Processed Tungsten Dichalcogenides
  • Small Methods (2024) – Knot Architecture for Biocompatible and Semiconducting Two-Dimensional Electronic Fibre Transistors

Sensors and Wearable Electronics

We develop wearable and printable sensing platforms using two-dimensional material networks. Applications include environmental monitoring, healthcare technologies, flexible strain sensors and electronic textiles capable of operating in real-world environments.

Selected publications

  • Scientific Reports (2022) – Washable two-dimensional material electronic textiles for NO₂ sensing
  • Nanoscale (2025) – Solution-processed negative gauge factor PtSe₂ strain sensors
  • ACS Applied Nano Materials (2025) – Exfoliated MoS₂ Nanosheet Networks as Sensing Materials for Nitrogen Dioxide Detection
  • Carbon (2017) – Environmentally-friendly conductive cotton fabric as flexible strain sensors

Charge Transport and Network Physics

Understanding charge transport in nanosheet networks is essential for the next generation of printed electronics. We investigate how nanosheet morphology, aspect ratio, junction resistance and electronic transport govern device performance, providing the fundamental design rules needed for scalable electronic technologies.

Selected publications

  • Nature Electronics (2021) – Charge transport mechanisms in inkjet-printed thin-film transistors based on two-dimensional materials
  • Nature Communications (2024) – Understanding how junction resistances impact the conduction mechanism in nanonetworks
  • Small (2025) – Using Electrical Impedance Spectroscopy to Quantify Junction and Nanosheet Resistance
  • ACS Nano (2025) – Quantifying the Influence of Nanosheet Aspect Ratio on Network Morphology and Junction Resistance
